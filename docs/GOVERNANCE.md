# Gishiki Governance

## Purpose

Governance exists to protect the integrity of Gishiki as a public-interest orchestration system.

The platform becomes more powerful only if it becomes more trustworthy.
This means governance must apply to:

- agents
- orchestration behavior
- partner participation
- monetization rules
- user-facing integrity

---

## 1. Human-first rule

The orchestrator's primary duty is to the human using the system.

This means:

- relevance outranks monetization
- clarity outranks volume
- safety outranks conversion
- trust outranks short-term revenue

No partner relationship should be allowed to overturn this rule.

---

## 2. One-surface rule

The human should face one coherent guide.

Governance should prevent the interface from collapsing into a swarm of visible competing agents, offers, or prompts.

Complexity belongs behind the scenes.
Coherence belongs at the surface.

---

## 3. Agent discipline rule

Each agent must have:

- a narrow scope
- explicit boundaries
- confidence signaling
- deference behavior
- feedback loops

No agent should be allowed to act as a totalizing authority.
Agents contribute perspectives, not absolute truth.

---

## 4. Partner fairness rule

The ecosystem should stay open, but trust must be earned.

This means:

- any compatible solution may enter as an open solution
- only proven solutions may become premium
- only premium solutions may pay for priority
- priority must remain subordinate to relevance

A company cannot purchase legitimacy.
It can only purchase advantage after legitimacy is earned.

---

## 5. Continuous review rule

No status is permanent.

Agents, workflows, and partners should all be subject to review based on:

- performance
- error rates
- user outcomes
- session coherence
- reliability over time

Governance should allow demotion, suspension, or retirement when trust declines.

---

## 6. Monetization boundary rule

Monetization is allowed only when it does not corrupt the logic of guidance.

This means:

- paid priority cannot force irrelevance
- partner money cannot rewrite the user's needs
- the system must not become a disguised ad network
- the public-interest layer must remain legible and credible

Revenue should support the ecosystem, not distort it.

---

## 7. Transparency rule

Where relevant, the system should make clear:

- what kinds of solutions are premium
- when a partner may have paid for priority placement
- what standard governs eligibility
- what the user can reasonably trust about a surfaced option

Transparency does not require exposing all internal reasoning.
But it does require honest signaling about system incentives.

---

## 8. Public-interest stewardship

If Gishiki is stewarded through a mission-driven structure, governance should protect:

- the open and accessible base layer
- the standards process
- the integrity of certification
- the separation between public-interest purpose and commercial pressure

The stronger the monetization engine becomes, the stronger this stewardship must become.

---

## 9. Practical governance questions

Every major product or partner decision should be tested against these questions:

- Does this improve the human's clarity?
- Does this preserve coherence at the surface?
- Does this reward proven quality over mere payment?
- Does this increase trust in the system?
- Would this still feel legitimate if fully explained to the user?

If the answer is no, the decision should be reconsidered.

---

## Final principle

> Gishiki should become a stronger market actor only by becoming a stronger steward of trust.
